Trump voters don't think what you think they think

Trump voters don't think what you think

A good analyst questions his assumptions. Continuously. That’s especially true when it comes to assumptions about Donald Trump, but it also applies to our understanding of the people who support him. On the one hand, Trump distinguished himself in a ginormous field of Republican candidates as an unapologetic hell-raiser. His refusal to give an inch carried him a thousand miles.

But Morning Consult/Politico published a national poll this week that we need to read closely. The survey found that 57 percent of Republicans said Trump should work with both Democratic and Republican lawmakers to get things done. Just 31 percent of Republicans said that Trump should work “primarily with Republicans,” and eight percent actually said he should work “primarily with Democrats.” Among respondents who voted for Trump in 2016, demand for a bipartisan approach was even higher. Some 64 percent said the president should work with lawmakers of both parties. Is that the Trump voter we think we know?

Then a new HuffPost/YouGov poll reported that 62 percent of Republicans and 69 percent of Trump voters approved of the president’s decision to side with Democratic leaders Chuck Schumer and Nancy Pelosi over the Republican leadership on a deal over managing US debt.

Assuming these polls accurately reflect something in the national mood, what do these numbers mean? Is this a fleeting moment of cooperative feeling that follows two natural disasters in the US in a year with no national election? Does it reflect public exhaustion with petty partisanship in Washington?

-Willis Sparks

Macron and Merkel in love

Those eager to see how the Merkel/Macron team will change Europe will see Angela Merkel elected this weekend to a fourth and final term as Germany’s Chancellor. But as Signal has noted before, Macron has lost a lot of political capital at home. The labor reform bill his party will soon pass might make things worse, at least at first. Merkel will need time to form a coalition that will define the contours of what Germany will do on Eurozone reform. And the leaders of France and Germany agree on less than some might hope. Creation of a single Eurozone budget and a European Monetary Fund are on the table, but Macron, Merkel, and other European leaders see the scale and purpose of these projects in very different ways. As ever in Europe, the devil is in the details.

Rohingya Muslims

What's going down:

More than 400,000 Rohingya Muslims have fled Myanmar in recent weeks in what they, and international observers, say is a campaign of ethnic cleansing by the Myanmar military. The military insists it is putting down a rebellion by Rohingya militants.

What they're saying

  • UN Commissioner for Human Right: This is horrible.

  • President Aun Sang Suu Kyi: im doing my best but let’s be serious, I don’t actually control the military what do you want?

  • Donald Trump: It was horrible and sad and very disgraceful the way the press treated me. And another thing, did you see what they said?

  • The Battersea Times: Aun Sang Suu Kyi has gone from nobel peace laureate to war criminal.

Why it matters:

the international community has always had a hard time getting together to address humanitarian disasters – the fragmentation of global leadership today makes that even harder. Who’s going to step up?

The Butterfly Effect

How a North Korea nuke can blow up your wallet

  1. North Korea tests an ICBM capable of hitting American cities

  2. The Trump administration blames North Korea’s closest ally, China, for not doing more to stop the nuclear program.

  3. The US government imposes stiff trade penalties on China, in line with President Trump’s earlier threats to get tough with Beijing on economic issues.

  4. Beijing responds by putting tariffs on American goods.

  5. The price of your electronics, bras, tires, and toys doubles.

  6. Kim Jon Un grins and points at things.
